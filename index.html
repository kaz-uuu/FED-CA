Marine Ecosystem Threats

Overfishing

HUMAN BEHAVIOR

Overfishing occurs when fish and other marine species are harvested at rates faster than they can reproduce. This depletes fish populations, disrupts food chains, and alters marine ecosystems. Overfishing not only threatens the survival of target species but also impacts bycatch species, including endangered ones. Sustainable fisheries management is crucial to prevent overexploitation, ensuring that fish stocks can recover and marine biodiversity is maintained. Effective policies, marine protected areas, and responsible fishing practices are essential to combat the negative impacts of overfishing on ocean health.

Ocean Acidification

CLIMATE CHANGE

Ocean acidification is a major consequence of increased carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. As the ocean absorbs more CO2, its pH decreases, making the water more acidic. This change in acidity adversely affects marine life, particularly organisms with calcium carbonate structures such as corals, shellfish, and some plankton species. Acidification can impair the ability of these organisms to build and maintain their shells and skeletons, leading to population declines and disrupting marine ecosystems. Additionally, the altered chemistry affects fish behavior and sensory functions, further impacting marine biodiversity.

Rising Sea Levels

CLIMATE CHANGE

Rising sea levels are primarily driven by global warming, which causes polar ice melt and thermal expansion of seawater. As glaciers and ice sheets melt, they contribute additional water to the oceans, while warming water expands in volume. This phenomenon poses significant risks to coastal communities and ecosystems, leading to increased flooding, erosion, and habitat loss. Coastal habitats such as wetlands and mangroves are particularly vulnerable, affecting the biodiversity and the livelihoods of millions who depend on these ecosystems. Furthermore, saltwater intrusion can contaminate freshwater resources, posing additional challenges for agriculture and drinking water supplies.

Marine Pollution

HUMAN BEHAVIOR

Marine pollution encompasses a wide range of harmful substances entering the ocean, including plastics, chemicals, and oil spills. Plastics are particularly pervasive, breaking down into microplastics that are ingested by marine organisms, leading to health problems and bioaccumulation up the food chain. Chemical pollutants, such as pesticides and heavy metals, can cause toxic effects on marine life, impacting reproductive and immune systems. Oil spills, although less frequent, have catastrophic short-term and long-term effects on marine ecosystems, coating wildlife and habitats with a suffocating layer of oil.
